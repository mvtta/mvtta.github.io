1. Mathematical Formalism

1.1. Causal Fermion System (CFS) Definition

A causal fermion system of spin dimension \[ n \in \mathbb{N} \] is a triple: \[ (\mathcal{H}, \mathcal{F}, \rho) \] where: - \[ \mathcal{H} \]: Complex Hilbert space (separable, possibly infinite-dimensional). - \[ \mathcal{F} \]: Set of all self-adjoint linear operators of finite rank on \[ \mathcal{H} \] with at most \[ n \] positive and \[ n \] negative eigenvalues (counting multiplicities). - \[ \rho \]: Borel measure on \[ \mathcal{F} \], called the universal measure[1][2].

Domain and Range: - \[ \mathcal{H} \]: Domain is the set of square-integrable spinor fields; range is the Hilbert space itself. - \[ \mathcal{F} \]: Domain is the set of self-adjoint operators; range is the operator algebra on \[ \mathcal{H} \]. - \[ \rho \]: Domain is \[ \mathcal{F} \]; range is \[ [0, \infty) \].

1.2. Causal Action Principle

The causal action is defined as: \[ S(\rho) = \iint_{\mathcal{F} \times \mathcal{F}} \mathcal{L}(x, y) \, d\rho(x) \, d\rho(y) \] where the Lagrangian \[ \mathcal{L}(x, y) \] is a function of the spectral properties of the operator product \[ xy \]:

\[ \mathcal{L}(x, y) = \frac{|| (xy)^2 || - \frac{1}{2n} |xy|^2}{|xy|^2} \] with \[ |xy| = \sum_{i=1}^{2n} |\lambda_i^{xy}|, \quad |(xy)^2| = \sum_{i=1}^{2n} |\lambda_i^{xy}|^2 \] where \[ \lambda_i^{xy} \] are the non-trivial eigenvalues of \[ xy \] (counting algebraic multiplicities)[1][3].

Parameters: - \[ n \]: Spin dimension, \[ n \in \mathbb{N} \], typically \[ n = 2 \] or \[ 4 \] for physical applications. - \[ x, y \]: Elements of \[ \mathcal{F} \]. - \[ \lambda_i^{xy} \]: Eigenvalues of \[ xy \].

Domain and Range: - \[ x, y \in \mathcal{F} \] - \[ \mathcal{L}(x, y) \in \mathbb{R} \] - \[ S(\rho) \in \mathbb{R} \]

1.3. Causal Structure

For \[ x, y \in \mathcal{F} \], the eigenvalues \[ \lambda_j^{xy} \] define the causal relation: - Spacelike: All \[ |\lambda_j^{xy}| \] equal. - Timelike: All \[ \lambda_j^{xy} \] real, not all equal in absolute value. - Lightlike: Otherwise[1][3].

4. Parameter Legends

  • n: Fractal or organizational level; integer; \[ n \geq 0 \]; origin: fractal hierarchy or scale.
  • D: Diffusion coefficient; real, positive; units: \[ \mathrm{m}^2/\mathrm{s} \]; origin: transport property of the medium.
  • T: Characteristic time; real, positive; units: \[ \mathrm{s} \]; origin: process or system timescale.
  • φ: Golden ratio; \[ \varphi = (1 + \sqrt{5})/2 \approx 1.618 \]; dimensionless; origin: self-similar scaling.
  • A: Agency/network capacity; real, \[ 0 < A \leq 1 \]; dimensionless; origin: coherence or distributed agency.
  • λ: Characteristic length; real, positive; units: \[ \mathrm{m} \]; emergent from scaling law.
  • Ψ: Coherence functional; real, positive; units: \[ \mathrm{m} \]; emergent from scaling law and agency.
  • x, y: Elements of \[ \mathcal{F} \]; self-adjoint operators; domain: operator algebra on \[ \mathcal{H} \].
  • ρ: Universal measure; measure on \[ \mathcal{F} \]; domain: operator space; range: \[ [0, \infty) \].

  • Origin: The formalism is rooted in the theory of causal fermion systems, fractal geometry, and universal scaling laws observed in quantum criticality, biological patterning, and information compression[1][4][6][3].
  • Domain: The parameters are defined on the set of natural numbers (for n), positive reals (for D, T, λ, Ψ), and the operator algebra of a Hilbert space (for x, y, ρ).
